Glasgow has a housing crisis

Glasgow City Council declared a housing emergency in November 2023. It was not a political gesture. The numbers behind it are stark, and they have not improved.

6,000+households with live homelessness applications
4,200+people in temporary accommodation every night
14,000+households on the social housing waiting list

The numbers

Glasgow's housing emergency is not a housing quality problem or a housing management problem. It is a supply problem. There are simply not enough homes for the people who need them.

Average rents for a two-bedroom home in Greater Glasgow have risen from around £730 per month in 2010 to around £1,275 today — a rise of 75% in fifteen years, far outstripping wage growth. The private rented sector has tightened dramatically: vacancy rates are near zero, meaning landlords face almost no competition for tenants, and renters face almost no alternatives.

The social housing waiting list has over 14,000 households on it. Glasgow City Council is using almost 1,500 bed and breakfast rooms for emergency placements — an expensive, inadequate, and deeply disruptive solution to a structural problem.

Glasgow has built fewer homes per capita than almost any comparable European city over the past three decades. The consequences — rising rents, long waiting lists, homelessness — are the predictable result of sustained undersupply.

This is not just a Glasgow problem

Scotland as a whole is building around 20,000 homes per year. Research from the Scottish Federation of Housing Associations, Shelter Scotland, and CIH Scotland finds that Scotland needs to build approximately 15,700 affordable homes per year alone — nearly double current affordable output of around 8,200. Across all tenures, Scotland needs closer to 25,000 homes per year to meet demand.

The Scottish Government declared a national housing emergency in 2023. Budget cuts to the Affordable Housing Supply Programme since 2023/24 — equivalent to a 22% real-terms reduction — have made the situation worse. Private sector housebuilding starts in 2024 were at their lowest since 2013.

What caused this?

Housing crises do not happen overnight. Glasgow's has been building for decades, driven by sustained undersupply across all tenures. The reasons for that undersupply — a planning system that makes building difficult and uncertain, regulatory requirements that add cost, a political culture that has treated housing as a local amenity to be protected rather than a public good to be expanded — are the subject of the pages that follow.

The important point is this: the crisis is real, it is serious, and it has a cause. Understanding the cause is the first step to fixing it.
