Glasgow has a housing crisis

Rents have risen 75% since 2010. House prices are up 70% since 2015. A city where ordinary people cannot afford to live is not a well-functioning city. The evidence is clear, and the cause is not complicated.

Rents have risen faster than wages

In 2010, the average monthly rent for a two-bedroom home in Greater Glasgow was around £730. Today it is around £1,275 — a rise of 75% in fifteen years. Over the same period, average wages in Scotland rose by roughly 40%. The gap between what people earn and what it costs to rent a home has widened substantially and consistently.

This is not a short-term fluctuation. The steepest rises came between 2021 and 2024, when rents jumped by over 30% in three years as the market tightened severely following a period of constrained supply. Glasgow's rental vacancy rate — the proportion of rental homes sitting empty at any given time — is close to zero. In a healthy market it would be 3–5%. Near-zero vacancy means landlords face almost no competitive pressure, and renters face almost no alternatives.

When a rental market has near-zero vacancies, it is not a market in any meaningful sense. It is a queue. And the people at the back of that queue — those on lower incomes, those without guarantors, those leaving temporary accommodation — have almost nowhere to go.

Buying a home has become harder too

House prices in Glasgow have risen by around 70% since 2015, from roughly £109,000 to £185,000 today. That sounds affordable by the standards of London or Edinburgh — and compared to those cities, it is. But the relevant comparison is not other cities. It is Glasgow wages.

Scotland's average full-time salary is around £38,000. At that income, and at current mortgage rates, a solo buyer can typically borrow around £152,000 — not enough to buy the average Glasgow home without a substantial deposit. Saving that deposit while paying today's rents is extremely difficult for most people. The result is that homeownership is being deferred or abandoned altogether by a growing share of the population, and the pressure on the rental market intensifies as a result.

This is not primarily a story about rich buyers pricing out first-timers. It is a story about supply: not enough homes are being built, so prices rise, so fewer people can buy, so more people rent, so rents rise too. The two markets are connected, and the shortage drives both.

Glasgow is not building enough homes

Scotland needs to build around 25,000 homes per year across all tenures to meet demand. It is currently building around 20,000. The gap — roughly 5,000 homes per year — compounds over time. Every year of undersupply makes the next year's shortage worse.

Glasgow City Council declared a housing emergency in November 2023. More than 6,000 households have live homelessness applications. Over 4,200 people are in temporary accommodation every night. The Scottish Government cut the Affordable Housing Supply Programme budget by 22% in real terms in 2023/24, making an already difficult situation worse. Private sector housebuilding starts in 2024 were at their lowest since 2013.

Why this matters for the argument

Glasgow's housing crisis is sometimes framed as a social housing problem: a question of waiting lists, homelessness applications, and council budgets. Those are real and serious. But framing it that way leads to a narrow set of solutions — more public funding, more social housing — which, while necessary, are not sufficient on their own.

The market-level evidence — rising rents, rising prices, near-zero vacancies — tells a different story. This is a supply problem. There are not enough homes for the people who want to live in Glasgow, across all income levels and tenures. Social housing waiting lists and private rents rise for the same underlying reason: the city is not building enough homes. That is what the pages that follow address.
