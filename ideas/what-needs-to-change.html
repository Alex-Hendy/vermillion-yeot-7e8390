What needs to change

Glasgow YIMBY makes specific, evidence-based demands of Glasgow City Council and the Scottish Government. Here is what we are asking for and why.

The housing crisis is not inevitable. It is the result of specific policy choices — and it can be reversed by making different ones. The changes below are not radical. They are the kinds of reforms that cities and countries with well-functioning housing markets have already implemented. What they require is political will.

Demands on Glasgow City Council
Demand 1

Set binding housing delivery targets in the Local Development Plan

Glasgow's Local Development Plan should include a binding annual housing delivery target, with a clear mechanism for monitoring and responding when delivery falls short. Aspirational targets that are never met are not targets — they are public relations. The LDP should treat housing as a priority, not one consideration among many.

Demand 2

Allocate more land for housing, particularly near transport

Glasgow should allocate significantly more land for housing in the next LDP cycle, with a strong presumption in favour of residential development within walking distance of subway and ScotRail stations. Higher density development near transport is good urban planning and good environmental policy. Glasgow's current approach consistently underallocates land in areas where demand is highest.

Demand 3

Remove parking minimums for development near public transport

Glasgow City Council should remove minimum parking requirements for residential development within 400 metres of a subway or mainline rail station. Requiring parking spaces in locations with excellent public transport adds cost, reduces density, and works against the city's own transport and climate goals. Car-free and low-parking development should be the default near transport nodes, not an exception requiring special justification.

Demand 4

Review the viability impact of affordable housing requirements

The 25% affordable housing requirement on private developments is well-intentioned but poorly calibrated. Glasgow City Council should commission an independent viability assessment of the cumulative impact of planning obligations on development in Glasgow, and be willing to adjust requirements on sites where the evidence shows they are preventing development from happening at all. A scheme that delivers no homes is worse for affordable housing than a scheme that delivers some.

Demands on the Scottish Government
Demand 5

Reform Section 75 to reduce delay and uncertainty

The Scottish Government should set statutory timelines for Section 75 negotiations and publish guidance on standard terms for common planning obligations. The current system, in which negotiations can drag on for years after planning permission is granted, is a significant barrier to delivery. The Housing Planning Hub is a start — but it needs to be backed by a fundamental simplification of the obligations framework.

Demand 6

Introduce a stronger presumption in favour of development

Scottish Planning Policy should include a stronger and more clearly enforceable presumption in favour of housing development where local authorities are failing to meet their housing land supply requirements. At present, the consequences of underdelivery are minimal. Councils that consistently fail to build enough homes should face meaningful pressure to change course.

Demand 7

Review the New Build Heat Standard in the context of housing supply

The Scottish Government should commission an independent assessment of the supply impact of the New Build Heat Standard, and consider whether the additional cost it imposes on new development — at a time of declared housing emergency — is proportionate to its environmental benefit. Given that new homes represent less than 1% of Scottish housing stock annually, the Standard's effect on overall housing emissions is minimal. Its effect on development viability is not.

Demand 8

Restore and increase the Affordable Housing Supply Programme budget

The Scottish Government should restore the Affordable Housing Supply Programme to at least its pre-cut level in real terms, and commit to a multi-year funding settlement that allows housing associations and councils to plan ahead. The 22% real-terms cut since 2023/24 has damaged long-term confidence in the programme and contributed directly to the fall in affordable housing completions.

These demands are the starting point for Glasgow YIMBY's campaigning work. If you want to help make them a reality, get involved.
