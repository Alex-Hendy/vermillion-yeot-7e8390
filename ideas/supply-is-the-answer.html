Building more homes is the solution

There is no example anywhere in the world of a city resolving a housing crisis without building significantly more homes. Public investment matters — but it cannot substitute for supply at the scale Glasgow needs.

Supply and demand works in housing

Glasgow's housing crisis has one root cause: demand for homes far outstrips supply. Rents rise when there are more people looking for homes than there are homes available. They fall — or rise more slowly — when supply meets demand. This is not a controversial economic claim. It is the same mechanism that governs the price of every other good and service.

Critics sometimes argue that housing is different — that building more homes does not lower prices, or only benefits the wealthy, or that the real solution is something other than supply. The evidence does not support these arguments. Our myths section addresses each of them in detail. The short answer is: cities that have built more homes have consistently seen rents rise more slowly, or fall outright, compared to comparable cities that have not.

Auckland upzoned 75% of its residential land in 2016. Rents rose 22% over the following eight years — compared to 34% nationally and 36% in Wellington. Lower Hutt acted alone, upzoning 80% of its land. Construction tripled. Rents fell 21% relative to comparable cities. Minneapolis, Austin, and others show the same pattern. The evidence is consistent and international.

Why public funding alone is not enough

Social and affordable housing is vital, and Glasgow YIMBY supports ambitious investment in it. But the scale of Scotland's housing shortage makes it impossible to build our way out through public funding alone.

Scotland currently builds around 8,200 affordable homes per year. Research from the Scottish Federation of Housing Associations and Shelter Scotland estimates the need at around 15,700 affordable homes per year — nearly double current output. Reaching that figure would require roughly doubling the Scottish Government's Affordable Housing Supply Programme budget, at a time when it has recently been cut by 22% in real terms.

Even if that budget were fully restored and doubled, it would still leave the market rate rental and ownership sectors untouched — the sectors where the majority of Glasgow's residents live, and where rents have risen 75% since 2010.

The only mechanism capable of operating at the scale required is private housebuilding. Not instead of social housing — alongside it. Every city that has made meaningful progress on housing affordability has combined public investment with a planning environment that allows private development to respond to demand.

International evidence — see our evidence page

Five cities that changed their planning rules and measured what happened. Auckland, Lower Hutt, Minneapolis, Austin, Croydon. In every case where supply increased significantly, rents rose more slowly or fell. In Croydon, where permissive policy was introduced and then reversed, delivery immediately collapsed. What about filtering — does new supply help people on lower incomes?

A common objection is that new market rate homes are too expensive for most people, so building them does not help those who need housing most. The evidence runs the other way. Research by Mast (2023), tracking the chain of moves triggered by new construction, found that vacancies cascaded down the income ladder — and the strongest rent reductions were in the oldest and cheapest homes. Renters at the bottom of the market benefit most from new supply at the top.

This is because new homes do not need to be cheap to make existing homes cheaper. They just need to exist — freeing up the homes below them in the chain, reducing competition, and giving renters alternatives they currently do not have.

Where we stand

Glasgow YIMBY supports social housing, mid-market rent, and all other affordable housing tenures. We also support market rate housebuilding, because the evidence shows it is the primary mechanism by which housing crises get resolved. Opposing one in favour of the other is a false choice that leaves everyone worse off.
