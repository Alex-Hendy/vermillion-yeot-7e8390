Demand 2 — Scottish Government
Reinstate the presumption in favour of sustainable development

Before NPF4, if a council was failing to deliver enough homes, the planning system forced it to say yes more often. NPF4 abolished this mechanism entirely. There is now no consequence for failure. It should be reinstated immediately.

What was the presumption?

Before NPF4, if a council could not demonstrate a five-year supply of effective housing land, its development plan was considered out of date. At that point, a mechanism called the presumption in favour of sustainable development kicked in. The balance of decision-making tilted in favour of approving housing — and to refuse, the council had to show that the adverse impacts of the proposal "significantly and demonstrably" outweighed the benefits.

This was an accountability mechanism. If you were not delivering enough homes, the system forced you to say yes more often. It created consequences for failure.

What changed?

NPF4 abolished the presumption entirely when it superseded Scottish Planning Policy in February 2023. There is now no consequence for a council that fails to deliver enough homes. No tilted balance, no relief valve, no self-correcting mechanism. A council can be 50% behind its housing target — as Stirling is — and face no pressure to approve additional housing.

This was not an accident or an oversight. The Scottish Government deliberately removed it. In 2020, after the Court of Session confirmed in the Gladman case that the tilted balance applied and that the bigger the housing shortfall, the more the balance should tilt towards approval, the government's response was not to fix the housing land supply problem. It was to consult on scrapping the presumption itself.

The effect is visible in the appeals data

16/18 planning appeals for housing dismissed since January 2025
~3,500 homes that would have been delivered by those dismissed appeals
27% planning appeal success rate in 2025 — a ten-year low

The Scottish Government's own research found that the presumption delivered between 5,500 and 8,000 consented homes over a five-year period when it was in force. Since its abolition, the picture has reversed. Between January and October 2025, 16 out of 18 planning appeals for housing were dismissed — proposals that would have delivered approximately 3,500 homes. Only 125 homes were "minded to be approved." Most of the dismissed appeals were in areas that had already declared local housing emergencies.

The Scottish Government's response to a court ruling that councils must approve more homes when they are failing to deliver was not to address the failure. It was to abolish the accountability mechanism. The system now has no stick — only aspirational carrot.

The government's defence does not hold up

In its February 2026 consultation, the Scottish Government acknowledged that the presumption delivered 5,500–8,000 consented homes, but argued that only "just over 1,000" were actually built, and that the mechanism generated "significant debate" and legal costs.

This argument is weak on two counts. First, the gap between consented and built homes is not a problem with the presumption — it is a problem with the wider planning and delivery system. Consented homes that are not built are not evidence that granting consent was wrong. They are evidence that other barriers — Section 75 delays, viability constraints, infrastructure requirements — are preventing delivery. We address this directly in our response to the landbanking myth.

Second, the fact that a policy generates legal debate is not a reason to abolish it. It is a reason to clarify it. The government's response to a functioning accountability mechanism was to remove accountability rather than improve the mechanism.

What we are asking for

The Scottish Government should reinstate the presumption in favour of sustainable development, so that when a council is failing to meet its housing targets, the balance of decision-making tilts in favour of approving new homes rather than refusing them.

This is not a radical proposal. It is a return to the policy that was in place from 2014 to 2023. It is what Homes for Scotland have been calling for since 2024. It is the minimum necessary to ensure that a planning system which depends on councils delivering enough homes has a mechanism for responding when they do not.

The mechanism is the same as for restoring windfall development: an amendment to NPF4, laid before and approved by Parliament.
