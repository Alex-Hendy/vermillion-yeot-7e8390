Demand 4 — Scottish Government
Reform Section 75

After a developer gets planning permission, they must negotiate a Section 75 agreement — an open-ended legal process covering affordable housing, infrastructure, transport, education, and more. There is no deadline. There are no standard terms. The negotiation can take months or years. This is a solved problem. Publish the rates, set a deadline, and let people build.

What is a Section 75 agreement?

A Section 75 agreement is a legal contract between a developer and the planning authority, setting out the developer's obligations in exchange for planning permission. These typically include affordable housing contributions, payments towards school capacity, transport infrastructure, flooding and drainage, open space, and public realm improvements. The agreement must be signed before building can begin.

Section 75 serves a legitimate purpose. It allows developments to proceed that would otherwise be refused — by ensuring the infrastructure impacts are mitigated. Developers accept this. The problem is not that contributions are required. The problem is that every contribution is negotiated individually, from scratch, on every site, with no standard terms, no published rates, and no deadline.

The scale of the problem

114 stalled housing sites identified across Scotland — over 20,000 homes delayed
13 yrs how long the Section 75 guidance went without being updated (2012–2025)
0 statutory deadlines for completing a Section 75 negotiation

The Scottish Government identified 114 stalled housing sites across Scotland, covering over 20,000 homes, where delivery had stalled for reasons including Section 75 delays. The Housing Planning Hub was created in 2025 specifically to tackle these blockages — an acknowledgement that the problem is serious enough to require a dedicated national intervention.

Until December 2025, the government's own guidance on Section 75 agreements dated from 2012 — thirteen years without an update. The replacement, Circular 4/2025, acknowledges the need to "streamline the negotiation process and speed up consenting." But updated guidance without binding timelines or standard terms is advice, not reform.

Why does each agreement take so long?

The LSE and University of Sheffield, commissioned by the Scottish Government to study Section 75, found that the negotiations are slow because multiple parties must agree on multiple obligations simultaneously. The council, the developer, and often education authorities, transport bodies, and utility companies must all sign off. Large complex schemes with multiple landowners are "particularly slow." The range of obligations has been expanding over time — what started as affordable housing and basic infrastructure now extends to education, transport, flooding, drainage, biodiversity, public realm, and more.

For smaller developers, the cost and uncertainty of Section 75 negotiations can be the difference between proceeding and walking away. The process rewards large firms with dedicated legal teams and deep pockets, and penalises the SME builders who should be delivering a larger share of Scotland's homes.

A developer who has spent years and hundreds of thousands of pounds getting through the planning process arrives at the point of getting permission — and then faces another open-ended negotiation before a single brick can be laid. Every month of delay costs money: site holding costs, financing charges, professional fees. The negotiation adds cost and delay without adding value, because the contributions it secures could be calculated in advance.

This is a solved problem

There is no reason why the standard obligations — education contributions, transport payments, affordable housing — cannot be published as a fixed tariff: so much per unit or per square metre, varied by zone if the economics differ across the city. The developer knows the cost on day one. The council receives the same contributions. The only thing that changes is that nobody spends months arguing about it.

This is not a theoretical proposal. Renfrewshire Council already does a version of it — they developed a template Section 75 agreement in collaboration with Homes for Scotland and several housebuilders, and prefer planning conditions over Section 75 wherever possible. Their timescales for securing developer contributions are significantly shorter as a result.

Edinburgh Council's own planners, in evidence to the LSE/Sheffield study, said that "a city-wide tariff or area-based tariffs could enable the council to be more proactive with infrastructure" and would "provide more certainty for developers and the public purse as well as reduce land speculation."

England has had a version of this since 2010 — the Community Infrastructure Levy, a fixed non-negotiable charge per square metre. Scotland considered introducing an equivalent levy under powers in the 2019 Planning Act, then decided not to proceed in November 2024. The result is that Scotland relies entirely on the negotiated Section 75 process — the slowest, most uncertain, and most expensive mechanism available.

What we are asking for

Councils should publish standard contribution rates for all common Section 75 obligations — education, transport, affordable housing, open space — set as a fixed amount per unit or per square metre, varied by zone if needed. Developers pay the published rate automatically. Section 75 negotiation is reserved only for genuinely site-specific matters that cannot be standardised.

A statutory backstop deadline should apply: if agreement on any remaining site-specific matters is not reached within a fixed period, standard terms apply automatically. The developer can proceed. The council receives its contributions. The homes get built.

The Law Society of Scotland has already recommended that councils publish model planning obligations with standard clauses. Edinburgh's own planners have asked for tariff-based contributions. Renfrewshire has shown it works in practice. The demand is to make the exception the rule — backed by statute.

LSE & University of Sheffield (commissioned by Scottish Government), 2021

Comprehensive study of Section 75 in Scotland. Found that speed is "affected by legal delays, and large complex schemes with multiple landowners were noted as being particularly slow." The range of obligations secured through Section 75 has been expanding, and the ease of negotiation "depends significantly on the clarity of local plans" — with "far less" clarity about non-housing infrastructure than about affordable housing. Renfrewshire Council — template Section 75 approach

Renfrewshire developed a template Section 75 agreement in collaboration with Homes for Scotland and several housebuilders. The council principally uses planning conditions rather than Section 75 to define infrastructure requirements, and has an Infrastructure Constraints Map identifying existing capacity so that contribution requirements are known in advance. Planning Circular 4/2025, December 2025

Replaced Circular 3/2012 after thirteen years without an update. Reflects NPF4, the housing emergency, and current market conditions. Seeks to "expand the use of viability assessments and improve procedure to streamline the negotiation process and speed up consenting." But introduces no statutory deadlines and no standard contribution rates.

Scottish Government infrastructure levy decision, November 2024

Despite powers in the 2019 Planning Act to create a Community Infrastructure Levy equivalent, the Scottish Government decided not to proceed.
