Demand 3 — Glasgow City Council
Set much higher housing targets

Glasgow's housing targets are calculated using a methodology that measures the consequences of the housing shortage and treats them as the baseline. The result is a target designed to perpetuate the crisis, not solve it.

The target is set from broken data

Glasgow's minimum all-tenure housing land requirement — the MATHLR — is 21,350 homes over ten years. Glasgow's new development plan, CDP2, sets its local housing land requirement at 22,569, barely above the minimum. This is the target for a city that has declared a housing emergency, with over 14,000 households on the social housing waiting list and more than 6,000 live homelessness applications.

The methodology behind these numbers is fundamentally flawed. The MATHLR is calculated by adding household projections to an estimate of existing housing need, then applying a flexibility percentage. Each component has serious problems.

The circularity problem

The household projections used to calculate the MATHLR are based on observed trends in household formation. But household formation is suppressed by the housing shortage itself. When there are not enough homes, people cannot form new households — so the projections show lower demand, which produces a lower target, which perpetuates the shortage.

This suppression takes many forms. Young adults live with their parents for longer because they cannot afford to rent or buy — 28% of people aged 20 to 34 in the UK now live with their parents, up from 25.6% a decade ago. A third of men in that age group still live at home. Couples who would separate stay in the same home because neither can afford to live alone. People remain in flatshares who would prefer to live independently. Others who would move to Glasgow for work never come because there is nothing available at a rent they can afford — and the household that would have formed in the city never shows up in the data.

As Professor Glen Bramley has observed: "If household growth has been artificially suppressed by the undersupply of new housing, then basing future need calculations on those lower growth figures will by necessity under-estimate that need." The MATHLR methodology does exactly this. It measures a population constrained by the housing shortage and treats it as the natural level of demand.

The housing shortage suppresses the very demand that should be telling us how many homes to build. Young adults who can't leave home, people who can't move to Glasgow for work, couples who can't afford to live apart — none of them appear in the household projections. The target is set based on a city that looks less popular than it actually is, because the people who would live there if they could are invisible in the data.

The existing need estimate is out of date

The existing housing need component of the MATHLR is based on homeless households in temporary accommodation and overcrowded or concealed households. But it uses 2011 census data and March 2020 homelessness statistics — years out of date. Glasgow's housing emergency has worsened significantly since then. The data that underpins the target does not reflect the scale of the crisis as it exists today.

The target is lower than what was already being built

Lichfields' research found that the MATHLR sets minimum housing delivery targets approximately 25% below the housing supply targets that were previously set within Local Development Plans across Scotland. The new national minimum is also below the rate of housing delivery in the years leading up to NPF4. The Scottish Government set a target lower than what was already being built — and called it a floor.

The site allocation problem

Even when targets are set, the sites allocated to meet them are concentrated in ways that shut out competition and slow delivery. Across Scotland, 55% of allocated housing units are on sites of 250 homes or more. Less than a quarter are on sites under 100 units. This shuts out the SME builders who historically delivered a significant share of Scotland's homes. Savills data shows that large housebuilders now account for 70% of registered new build transactions in Scotland, up from 42% ten years ago. The planning system has concentrated the market into fewer, larger hands.

CDP2 identifies 36 "Areas of Change" across Glasgow and aims to make land available for over 30,000 homes. But the approach is the same one that has consistently underdelivered: the council attempts to predict which specific sites should have homes and concentrates allocations on large strategic locations. Councils cannot predict market conditions. They do not know which sites will be viable, which will attract investment, or where demand will emerge.

What we are asking for

Glasgow City Council should set housing targets in CDP2 significantly higher than the MATHLR — on the understanding that not all allocated sites will be viable and not all will get built. The current approach of trying to allocate precisely the right number of homes in precisely the right places consistently fails because it depends on predictions that do not come true.

The alternative is to allocate generously. Set targets well above projected need. Include a much larger number and variety of sites, with a substantial proportion of small sites suitable for SME builders. Accept that some allocated sites will not be built. The ones that do get built will be the ones where the economics work — and the total number of homes delivered will be higher than under a system that tries and fails to pick winners.

Scottish Government NPF4 consultation analysis, November 2022

During consultation on the MATHLR, most respondents viewed the initial default estimates as low, requiring an uplift to reflect local circumstances and aspirations. Homes for Scotland proposed higher figures. The Scottish Property Federation called for upward adjustments. Colliers suggested 250,000 homes over ten years Scotland-wide.
