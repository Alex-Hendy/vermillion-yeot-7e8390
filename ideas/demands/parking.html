Demand 5 — Glasgow City Council
Abolish parking minimums

Glasgow requires one parking space per new home, plus 0.25 visitor spaces. This requirement has no evidence base, adds significant cost to every development, reduces the number of homes that fit on a site, and locks in car dependency. It should be abolished entirely.

What parking minimums do

Glasgow's Supplementary Guidance SG 11 requires a minimum of one car parking space per dwelling plus 0.25 visitor spaces per dwelling. This applies to new residential development regardless of location, proximity to public transport, or the actual car ownership patterns of the people who will live there.

The effects are straightforward. Each parking space takes up land that could be a home. Each structured parking space costs money to build — money that is added to the price of every home on the site. Each space encourages car ownership and use, working directly against Glasgow's own climate and transport goals. And the requirement is subject to discretionary negotiation, adding delay and uncertainty to a planning process that already takes more than double its statutory timescale.

The cost is real and significant

Research from Brookings, UCLA, and the Terner Center at UC Berkeley consistently finds that parking adds substantial cost to housing. Each structured parking space adds an average of £40,000–£80,000 in per-unit costs, depending on whether parking is above or below ground. Even surface parking consumes land that has an opportunity cost — every square metre used for a car is a square metre that is not a home.

These costs are not absorbed by the developer. They are passed on to residents through higher rents and purchase prices. UCLA researchers found that bundled parking increases rent by approximately 17%. On a Glasgow rent of £1,275 per month, that is over £200 per month in additional housing costs — paid by every tenant, including those who do not own a car.

Parking creates the car dependency it claims to serve

The argument for parking minimums is that residents need somewhere to park their cars. But the evidence shows the relationship runs in both directions: when parking is provided, people are more likely to own and use cars. Research by Millard-Ball and colleagues found that buildings with at least one on-site parking space per unit have more than twice the car ownership rates of buildings without parking, controlling for location and other factors.

This means parking minimums do not just respond to existing demand. They create demand. They lock in a transport pattern that Glasgow's own policies say it wants to move away from. Every parking space built today will be encouraging car use for decades.

Minneapolis eliminated parking minimums and single-family zoning in 2017. Over the following five years, the city's housing stock grew by 12%. Adjusted rents fell by 20%, while rents across the rest of Minnesota rose by 14%. No parking crisis materialised.

Cities across the world are abolishing minimums

Glasgow is behind the curve. Buffalo, NY became the first major US city to abolish parking minimums in 2017. Since then, dozens of cities have followed — including Minneapolis, San Diego, Austin, and Portland. California banned parking mandates near transit statewide in 2023. Washington State passed the strongest rollback of parking minimums in the US in 2024, capping most residential projects at 0.5 spaces per unit.

The results are consistent. Buffalo saw 53% less parking built after abolition — and 68% of new homes permitted since the reform would have been illegal under the previous code. No city that has abolished parking minimums has reported the parking chaos that opponents predicted.

NPF4 Policy 13 explicitly supports "developments which are ambitious in terms of low/no car parking in urban locations well served by sustainable transport." Glasgow's own SG 11 works against this national policy direction.

What we are asking for

Glasgow City Council should abolish minimum parking requirements for all new residential development. Developers — who bear the cost of building parking and the opportunity cost of the land it occupies — are better placed than planning officers to judge how much parking their customers need. Where there is demand for parking, the market will provide it. Where there is not, homes will be built instead.

This reform requires no new legislation. Glasgow can update SG 11 at any time. It would bring the city into alignment with NPF4 Policy 13, with international best practice, and with the direction every comparable city in the world is moving.
