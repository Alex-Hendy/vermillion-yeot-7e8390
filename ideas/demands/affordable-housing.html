Demand 6 — Scottish Government
Remove the 25% per-site affordable housing requirement

NPF4 Policy 16 requires at least 25% of homes on every private development to be affordable. On marginal sites, this kills schemes entirely, delivering zero homes instead of some. It is a tax on the one activity that could resolve the housing crisis: building more homes.

What is the 25% requirement?

NPF4 Policy 16, adopted in February 2023, requires that at least 25% of homes on any private development must be provided as affordable housing. This is a national policy, applied as a default across every local authority in Scotland. The Local Development Plan can set out circumstances where a lower contribution is justified, for example on viability grounds, but the 25% figure is the starting point for every scheme.

Why it reduces housing supply

The 25% requirement is a tax on new housing. It reduces the profitability of every private development by requiring a quarter of the units to be sold or rented at below-market rates. On sites where margins can absorb that cost, the scheme proceeds. On sites where land values are lower or construction costs higher, the requirement tips the scheme from viable to unviable.

The result on those marginal sites is not 75% market homes and 25% affordable homes. It is no homes at all. The affordable housing requirement prevents the construction of affordable housing, along with everything else.

A one-size-fits-all national rule does not work

The 25% requirement is set nationally in NPF4. It applies as a default to a site in central Glasgow with high land values and strong demand, and equally to a marginal brownfield conversion in a smaller town where viability is already stretched. It treats every council, every market, and every site the same.

Glasgow City Council has in practice been exempting private developments from the requirement, arguing that the city already delivers more than 25% affordable housing through its grant-funded Affordable Housing Supply Programme. Living Rent, the tenants' union, is campaigning for strict per-site enforcement. The national policy has created a fight that does not need to happen: a blanket rule that councils must either enforce to the detriment of housing supply, or work around at the cost of political and legal risk.

If you want subsidised housing, fund it properly

Scotland already has a well-established mechanism for funding affordable housing: the Affordable Housing Supply Programme, which channels Scottish Government grant to housing associations and councils. In 2024/25, Glasgow's AHSP delivered over 1,000 new affordable homes, funded by approximately £94 million in government grant. Glasgow's Strategic Housing Investment Plan identifies a need for £890 million from the Scottish Government over five years to deliver over 6,000 affordable homes.

The public subsidy route exists. It works. And it does not suppress market-rate supply. If the Scottish Government wants more affordable housing, it should fund it through general revenue, not through a targeted tax on the new construction that is the only mechanism capable of resolving the housing crisis.

The only solution to Glasgow's housing crisis is more homes. The only delivery mechanism capable of producing homes at the scale the city needs is market-rate housebuilding. A national policy that makes market-rate housebuilding harder does not help. It makes the crisis worse.

What we are asking for

The Scottish Government should amend NPF4 Policy 16 to remove the 25% per-site affordable housing requirement as the national default. Councils should be free to set their own affordable housing policies based on local evidence of need, viability, and what their housing market can sustain, without a national floor that suppresses supply on marginal sites.
