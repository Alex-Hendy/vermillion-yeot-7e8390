Demand 1 — Scottish Government
Restore windfall development

Between 2020 and 2024, two thirds of all consented homes in Scotland came from windfall sites — sites not allocated in a development plan. NPF4 Policy 16 shut this route down. Housing supply collapsed. It should be restored immediately.

What is windfall development?

Windfall development is housing built on sites that were not specifically allocated in the Local Development Plan. A developer spots a disused car park, a former office building, a gap site — and proposes housing on it. Before NPF4, this was how the majority of Scotland's homes got built. Between 2020 and 2024, windfall sites accounted for two thirds of all consented homes in Scotland. Only one in three came from sites allocated in development plans.

What changed?

NPF4 Policy 16, adopted in February 2023, effectively closed this route. Housing proposals on sites not allocated in the LDP are now only supported "in limited circumstances." A 2024 Court of Session ruling confirmed this interpretation.

Development plans have serious limitations. They take a decade to produce. They cannot anticipate where demand will emerge. The sites they allocate are often unviable. Windfall provided essential flexibility — it allowed the market to respond to demand by finding sites the plan had not predicted. Without it, Scotland's housing supply depends entirely on plans that are not fit for purpose.

The damage is already visible

56% fall in total housing consents since mid-2020 on an annualised basis
77 major housing applications determined in 2023/24, down from a ten-year average of 140
22/34 Local Development Plans in Scotland are now out of date

Housing starts have dropped from around 20,000 per year in the decade before NPF4 to just over 16,000 since its adoption. Total consents have more than halved, falling 56% on an annualised basis since mid-2020. By the first half of 2025, allocated sites accounted for 65% of consented homes — not because more allocated sites were coming forward, but because the windfall route that delivered the other two thirds had been shut off.

22 of Scotland's 34 Local Development Plans are now out of date. Only eight authorities have completed the evidence gatecheck required to progress their new plans. Thirteen will miss the May 2028 deadline. One has provided no timetable at all. The stock of viable allocated housing land is shrinking, with no mechanism to replenish it.

Since NPF4 was adopted, housing starts and completions have dropped back to levels last seen after the 2008 financial crisis. The planning system is restricting new supply at the very point when delivery needs to accelerate.

What we are asking for

The Scottish Government should amend NPF4 Policy 16 to reintroduce the ability to propose housing on suitable sites that are not allocated in a Local Development Plan. This would restore the route that historically delivered two thirds of Scotland's consented homes.

These projects would still require planning permission. What would change is that a housing proposal could no longer be refused solely because the council's plan did not anticipate it. In a declared housing emergency, we should be considering as many new projects as we can, not turning them away on a technicality.

The mechanism is straightforward. NPF4 can be amended by Scottish Ministers through a targeted amendment process, laid before and approved by Parliament. The political will is the only missing ingredient.
