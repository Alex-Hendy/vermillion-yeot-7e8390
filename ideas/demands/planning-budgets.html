Demand 6 — Scottish Government & Glasgow City Council
Double planning department budgets

Scotland's planning system requires individual permission for every new home. The teams that process those permissions are funded to miss their own deadlines by a factor of two. This has a direct cost: fewer homes get built.

The system demands individual permission for every home

Scotland's planning system requires every new home to go through an individual planning application, assessed on its merits by a planning officer, reviewed against national and local policy, and in many cases decided by a committee. This is a resource-intensive process. If the system insists on this level of scrutiny for every development, it should at least fund the teams that deliver it.

Planning departments across Scotland are handling live applications, writing new Local Development Plans, processing Section 75 agreements, discharging conditions, and responding to enforcement queries — all with the same staff. Glasgow's own planning service launched a three-year improvement plan in 2025 — an implicit admission that the service is not working.

Every week of delay has a cost

Developers must account for these delays before they begin. Every additional week in the planning process is a cost that must be factored into the financial appraisal of a scheme — site holding costs, financing charges, professional fees. On marginal sites, the additional cost can make the difference between a project that proceeds and one that does not. The result is fewer homes.

The delays also mean that only large developers with deep pockets can afford to absorb the time and cost of the planning process. Smaller builders — who should be increasing competition and bringing more diverse housing to market — are forced out. This is one reason why large housebuilders now account for 70% of registered new build transactions in Scotland, up from 42% ten years ago. The planning system's underfunding does not just slow delivery — it actively concentrates the market into fewer hands.

A planning system that takes more than double its own statutory timescale to make decisions is not a planning system that is working. It is a planning system that is rationing permission by delay — and the people who pay the price are the renters and buyers who need the homes that are not being built.

What we are asking for

The Scottish Government and Glasgow City Council should double the budget for planning departments. This is not an endorsement of the current system — Glasgow YIMBY's long-term goal is to move towards a rules-based system that would require far less individual assessment. But while the discretionary system exists, it should be funded to operate within its own statutory timescales.

Major applications should be decided in 16 weeks. Local applications should be decided in 8 weeks. These are not aspirational targets — they are the law. Whatever level of funding is required to meet them, that is what should be spent.

Scottish Government planning statistics, 2024/25

Major housing applications took an average of 37.7 weeks to determine — more than double the 16-week statutory target. Only 40% of local housing applications were decided within the 8-week target. Read the statistics →

Glasgow City Council, 2025 — Glasgow Planning Improvement Plan

Glasgow launched a three-year planning improvement plan in January 2025, with an update in January 2026. The plan covers staff development, process improvement, and digital innovation. Its existence is an acknowledgement that the service is underperforming — but it introduces no binding timescales for improvement.
