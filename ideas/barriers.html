The rules make it too hard to build

Scotland is not short of land, or ambition, or people who want homes. What it is short of is a regulatory environment that makes building those homes feasible. Here is what gets in the way.

The planning system's hidden effect

Scotland's planning system is often blamed for blocking development — but the most damaging effect is not the applications it refuses. It is the proposals that never get made in the first place.

Unlike zoning systems in the US or parts of Europe, where permitted uses are set out in advance and developers can invest with reasonable certainty, Scotland operates a fully discretionary system. Every application is assessed on its own merits against a Local Development Plan, Scottish Planning Policy, and whatever supplementary guidance applies. The outcome is uncertain until the decision is made — which can take years.

This uncertainty has a direct cost. Developers, architects, and landowners must spend significant money on pre-application consultations, environmental assessments, transport studies, and design iterations before submitting anything — with no guarantee of success. Smaller developers and self-builders, who cannot absorb that risk, simply do not participate. The chilling effect on supply happens upstream of the data we can measure.

Scotland built around 20,000 homes in 2024 — down 7% on the previous year. Private sector starts were at their lowest since 2013. This is happening during a declared national housing emergency. The system is not working.

Section 75 agreements

Once planning permission is granted, developers must negotiate a Section 75 agreement covering affordable housing contributions, infrastructure payments, open space requirements, and other planning obligations. These negotiations can take months or years, add significant cost, and introduce further uncertainty at a stage when the developer has already spent heavily on the application. The Scottish Government's own Housing Planning Hub was established in 2025 specifically to tackle delays caused by Section 75 negotiations — an acknowledgement that the problem is real and serious.

Affordable housing requirements

Glasgow City Council requires 25% of homes on private developments to be provided as affordable housing. This is a legitimate policy objective — but on sites where land values are marginal or construction costs are high, the requirement can tip a scheme from viable to unviable. The result is not 25% affordable homes and 75% market homes. The result is no homes at all. A policy intended to increase affordable housing ends up reducing overall supply, including the affordable element it was designed to protect.

Building energy standards

Scotland's New Build Heat Standard restricts the use of gas boilers in new homes, requiring more expensive low-carbon heating systems. This is a legitimate environmental goal — but it is being pursued in a way that is almost entirely self-defeating. Scotland builds around 20,000 homes per year, against a total housing stock of approximately 2.7 million. New homes represent less than 1% of stock annually. Even if every new home built from today met the highest possible energy standard, it would take decades to move the needle on Scotland's overall housing emissions. The existing stock — the Victorian tenements, interwar semis, and post-war housing where Scotland's energy problem actually lives — is unaffected. Imposing significant extra cost on new development to improve the energy performance of less than 1% of homes per year is not a serious environmental policy. It is a barrier to housebuilding dressed in environmental language.

Parking requirements

Many Scottish local authorities, including Glasgow, require minimum numbers of parking spaces per dwelling on new developments. In a dense city with good public transport — Glasgow has a subway network and extensive rail connections — this requirement adds cost, reduces the number of homes that can fit on a site, and works directly against the kind of compact, walkable development that reduces car dependency. It is particularly damaging near transport nodes, where car-free development is most appropriate and where the case for higher density is strongest.

The cumulative burden

Each of these requirements can be defended individually. Together, they make Scotland one of the most expensive and uncertain places in Europe to build homes. A developer assembling a site in Glasgow must factor in planning fees, pre-application costs, environmental assessments, Section 75 negotiations, affordable housing contributions, energy compliance uplifts, and parking provision — before a single brick is laid. On marginal sites, this cumulative burden is the difference between a scheme that proceeds and one that does not. Most of the time, it does not.
