Glossary

Plain-English explanations of the planning and housing terms used across this site. Scotland's housing debate has a lot of jargon. Here is what it means.

This glossary is a work in progress. More terms will be added regularly. If there's a term you'd like explained, email us at hello@glasgowyimby.com.

AHSP — Affordable Housing Supply Programme
The Scottish Government's main grant programme for funding the construction of affordable homes. Money flows through the AHSP to housing associations and councils, who use it to build social rent and mid-market rent homes. Glasgow's AHSP delivered over 1,000 new affordable homes in 2024/25.
Filtering
The process by which new homes at any price point benefit renters and buyers across the income spectrum. When someone moves into a new, more expensive home, they vacate a cheaper one. That vacancy cascades down the market until it reaches the oldest, most affordable stock. Research consistently shows the largest rent reductions from new supply are felt at the bottom of the market, not the top.
LDP — Local Development Plan
The document produced by each Scottish council that sets out where and what can be built in its area for the next decade. Planning applications are assessed against the LDP. 22 of Scotland's 34 LDPs are currently out of date.
New Build Heat Standard (NBHS)
A Scottish building regulation, in force from April 2024, that bans the installation of gas boilers in new homes. New homes must instead use zero direct emissions heating systems such as heat pumps. The standard adds significant cost to new development, particularly on sites where gas connections were previously the cheapest heating solution.
NPF4 — National Planning Framework 4
Scotland's national planning policy document, adopted in February 2023. NPF4 replaced the previous Scottish Planning Policy and sets the framework within which all planning decisions in Scotland are made. Glasgow YIMBY has significant concerns about two of its provisions: Policy 16, which restricts housing on sites not allocated in a development plan (the windfall route), and the removal of the presumption in favour of sustainable development.
Passivhaus
A German building standard requiring very high levels of insulation, airtightness, and mechanical ventilation with heat recovery. Passivhaus buildings use very little energy for heating and cooling. Scotland is introducing a Passivhaus equivalent standard for new homes, voluntary from 2026 and mandatory from 2028. The standard adds significant cost to construction.
Presumption in favour of sustainable development
A mechanism that existed in Scottish planning policy from 2014 to 2023. When a council was failing to deliver enough homes, the presumption tilted planning decisions in favour of approving new housing. NPF4 abolished it entirely. There is now no consequence for a council that fails to meet its housing targets.
Section 75 agreement
A legal agreement between a developer and a planning authority, required as a condition of planning permission. Section 75 agreements cover affordable housing contributions, infrastructure payments, open space provision, and other planning obligations. Negotiating them can take months or years, adding cost and uncertainty to development.
SHIP — Strategic Housing Investment Plan
A five-year plan produced by each council setting out how it intends to deliver affordable housing and what funding it needs from the Scottish Government to do so. Glasgow's current SHIP (2026/27 to 2030/31) identifies a need for £890 million from the Scottish Government to deliver over 6,000 affordable homes.
Windfall development
Housing built on sites not specifically allocated in the Local Development Plan. Before NPF4, windfall sites delivered two thirds of all consented homes in Scotland. NPF4 Policy 16 effectively closed this route, restricting housing proposals on non-allocated sites to limited exceptions. Housing starts have fallen sharply since.
YIMBY
Yes In My Back Yard. A direct response to NIMBYism — the tendency of people who already have homes to oppose the building of new ones. YIMBYs believe that more housing is good for everyone, that the people who benefit from high house prices and restricted supply should not have a veto over the city's future, and that the housing crisis will not be resolved without building significantly more homes.
