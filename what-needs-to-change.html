What needs to change

Glasgow's housing crisis is the result of a planning system that makes it too difficult, too slow, and too uncertain to build homes. Here is what we are asking for — reforms that can be made now within the existing system, and the longer-term changes needed to fix it properly.

Scotland's planning system is centrally planned in a way that few comparable countries would recognise. The Scottish Government writes a national framework. Councils write local plans that allocate specific sites. If a site is not in the plan, it effectively cannot become housing — regardless of how obvious the need. The plans take a decade to produce and are out of date before they are adopted. The result is a system that cannot respond to demand, and a housing crisis that deepens every year it remains unreformed.

What can be done now
Demand 1

Amend NPF4 Policy 16 to restore windfall housing development

NPF4 Policy 16, adopted in February 2023, effectively restricts new housing to sites already allocated in a Local Development Plan. This killed the "windfall" route — where developers could propose housing on suitable sites not in the plan — which historically delivered a significant proportion of Scotland's new homes. The Mossend court ruling in 2024 confirmed this interpretation. Planning approvals are down 66% since NPF4 was drafted. The Scottish Government should amend Policy 16 to reintroduce a presumption in favour of housing on suitable sites, whether or not they are allocated in a plan.

Demand 2

Set much higher housing targets in Glasgow's new development plan

Glasgow's current City Development Plan was adopted in 2017 — nine years ago. Its replacement, CDP2, is not expected until 2027 at the earliest. When CDP2 is adopted, its housing targets should be set significantly higher than projected need, with a much larger number and variety of sites allocated — including small sites suitable for SME builders. The current approach of trying to allocate precisely the right number of homes in precisely the right places consistently underdelivers, because councils cannot predict market conditions. Allocating generously gives the market room to respond to demand. The sites that get built will be the ones where the economics work.

Demand 3

Set statutory timelines for Section 75 negotiations

Even after planning permission is granted, developers must negotiate a Section 75 agreement covering affordable housing contributions, infrastructure payments, and other obligations. These negotiations are open-ended and can take months or years. The Scottish Government should set a statutory deadline for Section 75 negotiations — and if agreement is not reached within that deadline, a standard set of terms should apply automatically. The Housing Planning Hub established in 2025 is an acknowledgement that the problem is real. It needs to be backed by a binding timeline.

Demand 4

Abolish parking minimums for new residential development

Glasgow's Supplementary Guidance SG 11 requires a minimum of one car parking space per dwelling plus 0.25 visitor spaces. These standards are set arbitrarily, with no evidence base linking them to actual demand. They add cost to every development, reduce the number of homes that can fit on a site, and lock in car dependency at exactly the point when cities should be moving away from it. They are also subject to negotiation and discretionary interpretation, adding delay and uncertainty. Parking minimums should be abolished entirely. As public transport expands and car ownership patterns change, parking requirements set in concrete today will look increasingly absurd. Developers — who bear the cost of unsold or unused parking — are better placed than planning officers to judge how much parking their customers need.

Demand 5

Double the budget for planning departments

If Scotland insists on a system where every home requires individual planning permission, it should at least fund the teams that process those permissions. Major housing applications in Scotland take an average of 37.7 weeks — more than double the 16-week statutory target. Only 40% of local housing applications are decided within the 8-week target. Glasgow's own planning service launched a three-year improvement plan in 2025 — an implicit admission that the service is not working. Doubling the budget is the minimum required to bring decision times within the timescales the system itself demands.

Demand 6

Replace the per-site 25% affordable housing requirement with a city-wide target

Glasgow City Council currently requires 25% of homes on every private development to be provided as affordable housing. On marginal sites — brownfield, contaminated, or smaller developments — this flat requirement can make the entire scheme unviable, delivering zero homes instead of some. A city-wide affordable housing target achieves the same goal without killing individual developments. The council can meet the target through a mix of developer contributions, direct investment, and delivery on council-owned land — rather than requiring every single site to cross-subsidise affordable housing at the same rate regardless of whether the economics support it.

The long-term goal

The demands above work within the existing plan-led system. But the deeper problem is the system itself. Scotland attempts to decide in advance exactly which sites should have homes, exactly how many, and exactly when. When the plans are late — and 70% of Scotland's Local Development Plans are currently out of date — when allocations are in the wrong places, and when a single court ruling can freeze windfall development overnight, the entire housing supply depends on a process that takes a decade and consistently fails to keep up with demand. The long-term goal is to replace this with a rules-based system.

Replace discretionary planning permission with clear zoning rules

In a rules-based system, areas are zoned with clear rules on height, density, setbacks, and design. If a proposal complies with the rules, it can be built — no discretionary assessment, no committee vote, no negotiation. This is how housing works in Germany, Japan, and the cities that are actually building enough homes. It does not mean no rules. It means predictable rules that apply equally to everyone.

Zones near transport should permit dense housing by default

Areas within walking distance of subway and rail stations should be zoned to permit dense residential development as of right. The logic is obvious: people should be able to live near the places that connect them to work. This should not require special justification, negotiation, or departure from a plan. The zoning rules should already say yes.

Replace the development plan with a zoning map

The current model requires a comprehensive development plan — covering the entire city, every land use, every site — to be produced through a multi-year process of evidence gathering, consultation, examination, and adoption. Glasgow's current plan took years to produce and is now nine years out of date. The replacement will take at least ten years from start to finish. This is not a plan. It is a bottleneck. A zoning map that sets clear rules and can be updated as needed would achieve the same purpose without paralysing the housing supply every time the plan expires.

End third-party objections as a mechanism for blocking compliant development

If a proposal meets the zoning rules, neighbour objections should not be able to override the decision. Community input should inform the rules when they are being set — not block individual applications that comply with them. The current system gives a small number of vocal objectors disproportionate power over whether homes get built, at the expense of the much larger number of people who need those homes but have no voice in the process.

Abolish the requirement for individual planning permission for compliant developments

A development that meets the zoning rules should not need a separate political permission to proceed. Building warrants — covering structural integrity, fire safety, and energy standards — would still apply. But the question of whether housing can be built on a site that is zoned for housing should not be a matter for discretionary assessment. The answer should already be yes.
