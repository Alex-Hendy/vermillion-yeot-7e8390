Myth 3
"Rent controls are the answer to Glasgow's housing crisis."

Rent controls protect sitting tenants in the short run. The long-run evidence, accumulated over more than a century and across dozens of countries, is unambiguous: controls reduce supply, raise rents for new tenants, and make housing crises worse over time.

Why rent controls are politically appealing

The appeal of rent controls is easy to understand. Rents in Glasgow have risen 75% since 2010, far outpacing wages. Tenants feel squeezed. The proposal to cap rent rises sounds like direct relief — and for sitting tenants in controlled properties, it is, at least in the short run.

The problem is what happens next. When landlords cannot raise rents to market levels, the least financially viable ones sell up. Some properties convert to owner-occupation. Others leave the rental market entirely. The pool of available homes for rent shrinks. And a smaller pool, with the same or growing demand, means higher rents for the people who need to find somewhere to live — the very people rent controls were supposed to help.

This is not a theoretical prediction. It is one of the most consistently replicated findings in housing economics, documented across cities and countries for over a hundred years.

Supply constraints do not break markets — they make them function exactly as economics predicts. Rent controls reduce the returns on owning rental property, so some landlords exit. Fewer rental homes, same demand: rents rise. The control that was meant to make renting more affordable makes it, in the medium term, more expensive and more scarce.

The international evidence

Diamond, McQuade & Qian (2019) — San Francisco

The most cited modern study of rent control, using a natural experiment when San Francisco extended controls to small multi-family buildings in 1994. Rent control reduced rental housing supply by 15% as landlords converted properties to condominiums or redeveloped sites. The policy protected sitting tenants but transferred wealth away from future renters, who faced a smaller and more expensive market. Long-run rents rose citywide as a direct result. Read →

Kholodilin (2022) — 16 countries, 1910–2016

The most comprehensive cross-national study of rent regulation to date, covering over a century of evidence. Stricter rent regulation consistently reduces rental supply across all country contexts. Harder caps produce larger and faster landlord exits. The effect is present whether controls are introduced in wartime, peacetime, during boom periods or downturns — the supply response is robust across contexts. Read →

Sims (2007) — Massachusetts deregulation

When Massachusetts lifted rent controls in 1995, rental housing supply increased significantly — confirming that controls had been suppressing it. This natural experiment provides clean evidence of the supply effect because it ran in reverse: removing controls produced the increase in supply that economic theory predicts controls would reduce. Read →

Autor, Palmer & Pathak (2014) — Boston

After rent decontrol in Boston in 1995, property values increased substantially — not just in decontrolled units but in neighbouring uncontrolled properties too. The study estimated that rent control had been depressing property values and neighbourhood investment across the city, with the spillover effects of decontrol adding $2 billion in property value to the Boston housing market. Read →

Breidenbach et al. (2025) — US cross-city panel

Using machine learning to identify rent control reforms across 27 metropolitan areas in the US between 2000 and 2021, researchers found that more restrictive rent control reforms are associated with a 10% reduction in total rental units in a city. The effect was consistent across market types and geographies. Read →

Brookings Institution (2022) — comprehensive review

A review of the full body of economic evidence concluded that rent control "decreases affordability, fuels gentrification, and creates negative externalities on the surrounding neighbourhood." The review found broad agreement across economists — unusual in a field known for disagreement — that the long-run costs of rent control substantially outweigh the short-run benefits. Read →

What about the studies that show rent controls working?

The evidence is not entirely one-sided, and it is worth engaging with the strongest counterarguments. A 2022 study of rent controls introduced in Catalonia found rents fell 4–6% in controlled municipalities with no measurable reduction in the supply of rental units in the short term. A similar pattern was observed in some German cities.

These findings are real, but they need context. Short-term studies frequently miss the supply response, which takes time to materialise as landlords work through leases, decide whether to sell, and complete transactions. The San Francisco study found that the supply effect accelerated over years, not months. The Kholodilin analysis of a century of data consistently finds supply reduction over the medium and long term even where short-term studies find no effect.

The more important point is this: even if controls did reduce rents without reducing supply, they would not address the underlying problem. Glasgow's housing crisis is caused by insufficient supply. A policy that holds rents down without adding homes does not solve that. It manages a shortage rather than resolving it — and in doing so, it reduces the incentive to build the homes that would actually fix the problem.

Where we stand

Glasgow YIMBY understands why people support rent controls. When rents are rising fast and wages are not keeping up, direct intervention feels like the right response. But the evidence is clear: the policies that have actually made housing affordable — in Vienna, Helsinki, Auckland, Minneapolis — involve building significantly more homes, not capping rents. We want the same thing rent control advocates want: affordable housing for everyone in Glasgow. We just think the evidence points to a different way of getting there.

Who wins and loses under rent control
WINNING

Wealthier renters looking to buy

As landlords sell up, more properties enter the sales market. The people best placed to take advantage are those with savings or mortgage access. Rent controls quietly transfer housing wealth upwards.

MIXED

Sitting tenants

Renters already in a controlled property pay below the market rate in the short run — this is why controls are popular. But the protection is fragile and comes with a hidden cost: moving means losing your controlled rent, so tenants become trapped in homes that may no longer suit them. And if the landlord eventually sells, they re-enter a rental market that has shrunk.

LOSING

Renters in the cheapest homes

The properties most likely to leave the rental market are those at the bottom: older stock with the thinnest yields. When they go, the cheapest options disappear first — the exact opposite of the intended effect.

LOSING

New renters entering the market

A smaller rental market means more competition for fewer homes. Young people, those moving for work, and anyone leaving temporary accommodation face higher asking rents on the uncontrolled new-let segment and far less choice.
