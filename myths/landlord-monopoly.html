Myth 9
"Landlords operate as a monopoly."

Monopoly is a precise term with a precise meaning. The UK private rental market is one of the most fragmented industries in the economy — millions of independent operators, no dominant firm, no coordinated pricing. The monopoly framing misdiagnoses the problem and points toward the wrong solutions.

What a monopoly actually is

A monopoly exists when a single seller controls supply of a product or service, faces no meaningful competition, and can therefore set prices above the competitive level without losing customers. The key feature is market power: the ability to raise prices without triggering a switch to an alternative.

Classic examples are utility networks. Scottish Water supplies water to every home in Scotland. There is no competing water provider you can switch to if you dislike their prices. That is a monopoly. Network Rail owns the rail infrastructure. If you want to run a train from Glasgow to Edinburgh, you have one option for track access. That is a monopoly. The regulator has to set prices because competition cannot.

Now consider the Glasgow private rental market. There are thousands of individual landlords letting tens of thousands of properties across the city. The largest single operator controls a fraction of a percent of available stock. When a tenant is priced out by one landlord, they can and do approach dozens of others. There is no coordination, no price-fixing agreement, and no single entity capable of withholding supply. That is not a monopoly by any conventional definition.

How fragmented the market actually is

English Private Landlord Survey 2024 — DLUHC

There are approximately 2.8 million private landlords in England alone. 45% own just one rental property. 83% own between one and four. The ten largest institutional landlords together account for fewer than 1% of all private rental homes. No landlord or group of landlords has the market share to set prices independently of competition.

Scottish Government Private Sector Rent Statistics, 2024

Scotland's private rental sector is dominated by small individual landlords. Registered landlords in Scotland number over 220,000, and the overwhelming majority own fewer than five properties. The sector is structurally incapable of monopoly pricing: there are simply too many independent participants, each making their own decisions, for any form of coordinated market control.

Compare this with what economists call a highly concentrated market. The UK supermarket sector, for example, has four firms controlling roughly 70% of all grocery sales. Even that is not considered a monopoly — it is an oligopoly, and it still faces meaningful competition. The private rental market is far more fragmented than any sector that antitrust regulators would regard as concentrated.

What monopoly power actually looks like, versus what we have

True monopoly
Scottish Water, Network Rail, Royal Mail delivery
  • Single or near-single provider
  • No substitute product available
  • Prices set without competitive constraint
  • Regulated by government because market cannot discipline
  • Customer has no meaningful exit option
Competitive market
Glasgow private rental sector
  • Thousands of independent providers
  • Direct substitutes exist across the city
  • Prices constrained by competition between landlords
  • No coordination possible at this scale of fragmentation
  • Tenants can and do switch between providers

The experience of renting in Glasgow feels coercive, and it is — but that coercion comes from scarcity, not from monopoly power. When supply is severely restricted, every seller in the market gains pricing power, even when they are competing with each other. The relevant constraint on a Glasgow landlord is not the absence of competition: it is the absence of homes. Those are different problems with different solutions.

The "effective monopoly" argument: compelling but wrong

A more sophisticated version of the claim holds that even if landlords are technically competitive, housing is a necessity without a substitute, so landlords collectively wield monopoly-like power over tenants. If you need somewhere to live, you cannot simply opt out of the rental market. In that sense, tenants face coercion regardless of how many landlords there are.

This argument has real emotional force, but it conflates two separate things: the inelasticity of demand for housing in general, and monopoly power in a specific market. Water is also a necessity with no substitute — but the answer to expensive water is not to accuse water retailers of being a monopoly; it is to ensure that supply is adequate and infrastructure is accessible. The same logic applies to housing.

The inelasticity of demand for housing does mean that the consequences of undersupply are severe. People cannot easily reduce their consumption of housing when prices rise, so a shortage produces acute human harm. But the mechanism is still supply and demand, not monopoly pricing. The solution is still to build more homes — not to regulate landlords as if they were a utility, which addresses only the symptom while the underlying shortage remains.

Competition and Markets Authority — rental market analysis

The CMA has never designated the UK private rental sector as a monopoly or oligopoly. In its 2023 and 2024 housebuilding market studies, the CMA identified the planning system and land supply constraints as the primary causes of high housing costs — not landlord market concentration. Enforcement action against individual landlords for price gouging has been minimal, because the evidence does not support a finding of coordinated or monopolistic conduct.

Why this framing leads to the wrong policy conclusions

If landlords are a monopoly, the logical policy response is monopoly regulation: rent controls, licensing restrictions, caps on returns. Some of these tools may have merit in narrow contexts. But framing the problem as monopoly power leads directly away from the actual solution — building more homes — and toward interventions that address pricing without addressing supply.

Glasgow has direct recent experience of this. Scotland introduced emergency rent controls in 2022 under the Cost of Living (Tenant Protection) Act, later transitioning to a more permanent framework. During and after this period, the supply of private rented homes in Scotland fell, as some landlords sold up or shifted properties to short-term lets. Rents on newly available properties rose sharply as the remaining supply tightened further. Existing tenants in controlled tenancies gained some protection. New tenants entering the market faced worse conditions than before. This is the predictable outcome of treating a supply problem as if it were a monopoly problem.

Propertymark / Zoopla rental market data, Scotland 2023–2024

Following the introduction of rent controls in Scotland, the number of homes available to rent fell significantly. Propertymark reported that average available properties per letting agent branch in Scotland fell to historic lows in 2023 and 2024, while advertised rents on newly listed properties rose faster in Scotland than in England. The controlled market shrank; the uncontrolled margin of new lettings became more expensive. A supply shortage treated as a monopoly problem became a worse supply shortage.

What should we say instead?

Glasgow's rental market is not a monopoly. It is a severely undersupplied competitive market in which thousands of independent landlords price their properties according to what the market will bear — and the market bears very high rents because there are not enough homes. The problem is scarcity, and the solution is supply.

Describing landlords as a monopoly feels satisfying because it provides a clear villain and implies a clear remedy. But it is descriptively wrong, and it reliably produces policies that harm the very tenants it claims to protect. Understanding the actual mechanism — competitive pricing under conditions of severe scarcity, driven by a dysfunctional planning system — is a precondition for fixing it.

Where we stand

YIMBY Glasgow is not in the business of defending landlords. High rents cause real harm to real people in this city, and the political anger behind the monopoly claim is entirely justified. But the claim is wrong, and policies built on it have consistently made things worse. The planning system restricts supply, scarcity gives all sellers pricing power, and rents rise. That is the mechanism. The answer is more homes, faster — not the misapplication of monopoly regulation to a fragmented competitive market.
